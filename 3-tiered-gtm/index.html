Three-Tier GTM Strategy

How a single ingredient becomes a category, a contract, and a CMO stake.

Hero ingredients enable claims on packaging. Branded ingredients carry recall value even after the molecule commoditises — the end customer learns the brand, asks for the ingredient by name, and pays a premium for it.

The premise, in one number

The clearest recent proof is David Protein. A single proprietary input — EPG at 0.7 kcal/g versus 9 kcal/g for fat — let a 2024 startup hit 28g protein in 150 kcal, pushing calories-from-protein to ~75% in a category stuck around 35–40%. That number alone took David from zero to ~$180M in year one, with $300M+ guided for 2026.

The bar is incidental. The asset is the molecule. David’s Series A bought Epogee outright, cut off every other buyer, scaled EPG capacity 5×, and now sells EPG to legacy CPG on long-term contracts. Our GTM compounds on the same logic and splits across three customer archetypes — sequenced so the early wins fund the longer bets.

The Architect Model

Gen 2 industrial bio (Zymergen, Amyris, Ginkgo, Perfect Day) erased −80% to −100% of public-market value by trying to be platform, ingredient supplier, and consumer brand inside one P&L. We deliberately do not build platform horizontals or accumulate fixed-cost teams ahead of demand. Pick narrow problems large companies will pay to de-risk. Iterate through CROs and external formulators. Scale only what’s validated. Capture defensibility in scale-up trade secrets and partial CMO ownership.

Who

Funded D2C and category-disruptor brands with distribution from Day 0. The flagship channel is The Foundery — Nikhil Kamath and Kishore Biyani’s program pairing high-agency founders with built-in retail and trade access. The goal is to become the de-facto innovation partner for nutrition and CPG cohorts. At least one breakout per cohort is enough.

The play

End-to-end formulation partner with our ingredient as a hero on the pack. The brand’s growth becomes the ingredient’s growth. The best case is escape velocity, with two acceptable exits:

  • Acquisition. A legacy player buys the brand (Huel → Danone), pulling our ingredient onto a much larger distribution surface.
  • Buyout. The brand acquires the ingredient outright with guaranteed off-take. David → Epogee → EPG to legacy CPG is the canonical sequence.

Either way, we own the input. A legacy player cannot reformulate around an input they do not control.

Engagement model

Formulation credits, borrowed from the API-credit playbook of AI labs. We absorb the first formulation cycle plus three feedback rounds at no cash cost. These brands have urgency, not R&D budgets. “End-to-end” means the science and the design — Uday on our payroll lets us own packaging real estate, not just the ingredient inside it. Equity in the challenger brand is case-by-case, not a precondition.

Ingredient criteria

Existing, regulator-approved, no cost-per-kg ceiling. Reputational risk for the brand is low here, so we can take deliberately bold swings post-relationship — GMO strains, peptides — once the first easy win lands. Bold bets are expensive on our side; we earn the right to make them.

Anchor project

Yashu’s longevity-hydration brand under The Foundery. Water-based beverage in an aluminium can, electrolytes plus actives, with powdered supplements and effervescent tablets to follow. The SuperYou playbook — accessible product as the wedge, higher-ASP supplements for margin. Evian-inspired positioning. Longevity actives commercialised by DSM-Firmenich are lead candidates; Pruv® DHA-15 regulatory to be scoped before commitment.

Who

Multi-generational consumer brands funded by debt and customers, not venture. Strong cash flows, deep operational nous, no R&D infrastructure. There is no recurring need for a scientific team with novel ingredient expertise — all they need is a regular formulation R&D team at market rates. Anand Sweets (Ankush Dadu, second-gen, ~20,000 kg mithai/day, ~20,000 kg ghee/month) is the prototype.

The play

Same hero-ingredient logic, different motivation. They want claims their customers respond to — less sugar, lower GI, higher protein, fiber, gut health — without staffing a $100k formulation study. We do the formulation at cost; we make our money on the ingredient sale. Once the first product moves, two things compound:

  • Direct competitors come to us. The ingredient is novel in the category, and we hold the only application know-how for it. Competitor pull-through is structural.
  • We earn the right to charge upfront fees on later projects, plus recurring ingredient margin. Even after the molecule commoditises, the branded recall keeps the premium.

The Indian mithai market alone is ₹80,000 cr. Mithai players know their existing inputs deeply but do not know how novel ingredients behave alongside ghee, milk, and sucrose at scale. That gap is the wedge.

Two positioning modes

  • For commodities already used in the category (allulose, stevia), engineer a proprietary blend and brand the formulation.
  • For commodities that haven’t crossed over (PHGG is established in supplements, absent in F&B), brand the ingredient outright.

The identity wedge

The next-in-line heir watches school and college peers in venture, funded startups, and banking — sexier resumes than the family business, regardless of cash flow. Partnering with a science-backed, NUS-research-driven, venture-funded startup closes most of that gap.

Many won’t fund R&D from the company P&L, but they’ll write a personal CVC check — a great signal and a two-way channel. They get a window into the future of ingredients; we get ground-truth problems.

Ankush is also vocal about claims integrity — the Diabliss “sugar-free” sham is exactly the failure mode he refuses to repeat. That moral floor is a feature, not a friction.

Anchor: Mysore pak with rare sugars

Sugar in mysore pak does four jobs at once: sweetness, bulk, the crystal scaffold for the signature porous fracture, and the Maillard browning. Standard reformulations only solve sweetness. Our blend: ~40% sucrose retained for nucleation; D-Tagatose for browning (C-4 epimer of fructose, 1.5 kcal/g, GI ~3, prebiotic via colonic SCFA fermentation); allulose for sweetness top-up and to keep the block from going brittle; micro-dose monk fruit for parity.

Who

Global CPG and ingredient majors — L’Oréal, Reckitt, Mars Petcare. Long cycles, strict standards, deep pockets, off-take agreements that move the needle.

The play

In-house development of high-risk, high-reward, $Bn-TAM ingredients, co-developed with a legacy anchor that funds the Minimum Desirable Product (MDP — desirability validated by paid pilots before viability or feasibility get any further investment). The anchor’s off-take agreement de-risks the build before we commit to scale-up. Phased over a ~3-year development and sales cycle. After de-risking, we take partial ownership of the manufacturing facility — these are the ingredients that become staples on trade value and volume.

Big brands will not print “powered by Anomaly” the way a challenger will. They reformulate constantly to stop incumbents from cloning them — to them the ingredient is IP, not marketing. That’s why challenger and family-business tiers come first: they build the recall value that forces the legacy conversation.

Defensibility

Scale-up trade secrets — fermentation conditions, downstream recovery, QC protocols — and the off-take contract itself. Patents can be important.

Why GMO is deliberately not the play

A GMO ingredient locks the addressable market to the US, and within the US, to family businesses rather than the legacy players. Berkeley Yeast — replacing hops by teaching brewer’s yeast to express the terpenoid — is the reference. Real business, US-only, not a $Bn one.

Ingredient criteria

Regulatory moats. ~3-year approval timelines in major markets. The timeline is the moat.

Default-alive is the unlock

Tier 1 and Tier 2 cash flows fund the wait. The discipline is explicit in A Default-Alive Biomanufacturing Company — no platform-company posture, no premature in-house manufacturing, no parallel-build problem. Modular CDMOs, completed delivery cycles, then equity stake. Rinse and repeat.

Anchor project

Pawsible® Protein with Mars Petcare. Second paid pilot at $150k, on track to hit pre-commercial volumes next year. Mars carries the regulatory cost; we carry the process IP.

What comes next

Once Samyak and I have closed one full cycle in each tier, the deals become playbooks. We then hire low-ego, high-agency operators to run them in parallel — across stakeholders and geographies.

The thesis only works if the first cycle in each tier is run by founders. After that, it is a hiring and process problem, not a strategy problem.
